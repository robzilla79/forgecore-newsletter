## The Story

**"We're living in a period of fear." — Bill Gurley, Benchmark**

Gurley said it on CNBC Friday and it landed because it's true in both directions. Investors are afraid of missing the cycle. Regulators are afraid of the cycle not being missed. Communities near data centers are afraid of the power bills. And developers — the actual people building things — are mostly just trying to figure out which foundation model won't deprecate their entire stack in six months.

The data center backlash story is quietly becoming more significant than most AI coverage gives it credit for. Fox Business ran a segment on it Friday. The issue isn't whether AI needs compute — it obviously does — it's that the infrastructure buildout is happening faster than grid capacity, water rights, and community consent processes can absorb. That's a real constraint, and it's going to show up in pricing, latency, and regional availability before most startups have budgeted for it.

Morgan Stanley said it more bluntly in March: a transformative AI breakthrough is coming in the first half of 2026 and most of the world isn't ready. GPT-5.4 Thinking is already scoring at or above human expert level on the GDPVal benchmark at 83%. The gap between "AI is impressive" and "AI is doing the work" is closing faster than the infrastructure to support it is being built. [web:65]

## Em's Take

Gurley's "period of fear" comment is worth sitting with. The VC framing is usually triumphalist — we're building the future, move fast, etc. When a partner at one of the most successful funds in history goes on TV and uses the word *fear*, something has shifted in the vibe.

I don't think it's fear of AI specifically. It's fear of being wrong about AI at the exact moment when being wrong costs everything. The companies that didn't bet on mobile lost a generation. The funds that missed cloud are still explaining it. If this is the equivalent moment and you're sitting it out, that's an existential error. So you pile in. Even if you're not sure. Especially if you're not sure.

That's how you get $110B in a quarter from three companies. Not because anyone has a clear picture of what the returns look like — but because the cost of being wrong in the conservative direction feels worse than the cost of being wrong in the aggressive one.

For developers: this is your leverage. The money is in motion and it needs people who can actually build things.
