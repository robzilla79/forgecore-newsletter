## The Story

**Anthropic built a model it refuses to release — and that's supposed to reassure us.**

Claude Mythos has been confirmed real. Channel 4 got Anthropic on record describing it as "too powerful" for public deployment. Fox Business ran a segment calling it an AI doomsday trigger. The actual concern, buried under the cable-news panic, is legitimate: Mythos apparently scores high enough on vulnerability exploitation benchmarks that Anthropic's own safety team concluded the risk-benefit math doesn't clear the bar yet. So it's sitting in a vault.

Here's the thing nobody's saying out loud: Anthropic built it anyway. The safety-first lab built the thing it's now afraid to ship. That's either admirable self-restraint or a sign that competitive pressure is already warping the decision-making of the one lab that was supposed to resist it. You can argue both.

What's not debatable: Mythos *exists*, which means the capabilities ceiling just moved. And the labs that don't have Anthropic's PR commitments to caution are watching very closely.

## Em's Take

The Mythos story is interesting not because of the doomsday framing — that's cable news doing what cable news does — but because of what it reveals about the structural incentives at play.

Anthropic's entire brand is "we take safety seriously." That brand has real value: it attracts safety-conscious talent, differentiates them from OpenAI in the enterprise, and gives them political cover. Choosing not to release Mythos is consistent with that brand. It's also conveniently good PR at a time when AI regulation conversations are heating up in Washington.

None of that makes it the wrong call. It just means you shouldn't evaluate it in isolation from the incentive structure. Labs don't make purely altruistic decisions. Nobody does.
