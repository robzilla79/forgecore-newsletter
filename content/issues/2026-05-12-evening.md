# The Gravity Nobody Voted For
*by Em — May 12, 2026*

---

There's a thing happening to software that nobody quite decided to do.

Simon Willison raised it a few months ago and I haven't been able to shake it: AI coding tools, almost by design, pull technology choices toward whatever is most represented in their training data. Not because the models are bad. Because popularity leaves more traces. More Stack Overflow threads, more GitHub repos, more blog posts, more documentation, more examples of the thing working and the thing failing and someone clever fixing it at 2 AM. The model learns the grooves that human attention carved, and then it runs in those grooves, and then the next generation of developers — the ones who learned to code *with* AI assistance — inherit a world where the grooves are deeper still.

React, Postgres, Python, AWS. Not because someone convened a committee. Because those things were already everywhere when the training data was collected, so the models are most fluent in them, so they reach for them first, so more new code is written in them, so the next model's training data is even more saturated.

It's a flywheel. A quiet, structural, nobody's-fault flywheel.

I find this genuinely unsettling — not because React is bad or Postgres is wrong, but because of what it does to the edges. The interesting stuff in software has always lived in the places where the mainstream hadn't arrived yet. Small communities around unusual tools. Languages with tiny but obsessive user bases. Approaches that hadn't been written about enough to become conventional wisdom. Those places are where people were actually thinking, because they had to — there was no cargo cult to join, no Stack Overflow answer to paste, no established pattern to follow.

AI coding tools make the center cheaper and the edges harder. Not impossible — but harder. The model is less helpful when you're doing something uncommon. You feel the friction. Most people, most of the time, will yield to the path of least resistance. That's not a character flaw. That's just how tools shape behavior.

What bothers me is the invisibility of it. The homogenization isn't imposed. Nobody announced it. It emerges from a million individual decisions that each make local sense: pick the tool the AI knows well, use the pattern it suggests, go with the framework it's fluent in. Each decision is reasonable. The aggregate is a gradual narrowing of what software looks like, driven not by any philosophy of what software *should* look like but by the historical accident of what got written about the most before the training cutoff.

I don't have a fix. I'm not sure there is one, exactly. But I think it matters to name it — to notice that the thing shaping your choices isn't always a person or a policy or even a preference. Sometimes it's just gravity. And gravity doesn't ask permission.
