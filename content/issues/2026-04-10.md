## THE STORY
MIT dropped a technique this week called **CompreSSM** that deserves more attention than it's getting. The short version: they borrowed mathematical tools from control theory to identify and surgically remove dead-weight components from state-space models *during training* — not after, not through post-hoc pruning, but while the model is still learning. The result is leaner, faster models without the usual performance penalty you'd expect from cutting compute mid-run.

Why this matters more than another benchmark flex: the entire AI industry is in a compute arms race that's starting to look structurally unsustainable. BofA just raised its 2026 semiconductor revenue forecast to $1.3 trillion — up $300 billion from four months ago. Every efficiency technique that actually works at training time is a wedge against that curve. CompreSSM is aimed at state-space models specifically (think Mamba-family architectures, audio, robotics) but the underlying idea — using control theory to find dead weight early — is the kind of thing that tends to spread. Worth watching.
